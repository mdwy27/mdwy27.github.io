Last summer, I decided to take on a project that was, in retrospect, probably beyond my skill level at the time: building an autonomous ground robot with a digital twin using ROS2 and SLAM. I had just finished my second year of a physics degree. I'd written some code before, but mostly data analysis scripts for physics lab experiments. I had never once typed a command into a terminal. But I figured the best way to learn was to dive into a project and learn as I went.

I borrowed a laptop from a peer who had expressed interest in collaborating, and got to work. Over a few weeks I was doing things that felt completely foreign like SSHing into a Raspberry Pi, installing ROS2 Humble, setting up workspace directories, importing libraries, and writing my first C++ and Python nodes. It’s funny to remember being nervous typing into the terminal, I was convinced I was one wrong command away from breaking the computer. I even procrastinated for a few days because I worried I wasn’t experienced enough for the project. But eventually, I realized I would become experienced through the project, and this thought motivated me to simply keep “doing”. I’ve carried that mindset with me since then, as I continue to work on this project, that I will simply make very many mistakes and figure out how to fix them as they come.

I was proud of the progress over the summer. For someone who was simultaneously taking their very first *official* Python course, I was excited to get to work on a real, applied project. Getting to see some of the real applications of learning programming and robotics made me all the more motivated to show up to class and work on my coding skills.
Then one day I went to the lab where we'd been leaving the laptop, and it was gone. After a few days of confusion, I found out my peer had transferred schools and taken the laptop with him — along with everything I'd built on it. Just like that, the workspace I had built and the code I had been working on was gone.

I won't pretend I wasn't frustrated. Suddenly, I had to come up with a plan for how to keep this project going. After thinking about it, I realized I had to take more ownership over my work moving forward. The following fall, I bought my own laptop, started over from scratch, and rebuilt everything. Luckily, everything was much faster this time, because I'd already done it once before. I also made a mental note to never let my work live in only one place again (Hence this GitHub repo. I am trying to work on developing better habits when it comes to documenting my work :) )

Looking back, losing that work wasn’t all bad, though it did feel like a real setback at the time. It led me to take direct responsibility for keeping this project going, and rebuilding from scratch meant I gained a deeper understanding of what I was doing the second time around. This time, I tried to recall what I had to do and apply what I had learned the last time, rather than just directly following steps from a tutorial. Ask me to do it a third time and I’m sure it will be a breeze.
